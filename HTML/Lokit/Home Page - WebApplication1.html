A year has passed since the release of the famous Disney film Stitch. One of the main characters of the legendary cartoon was the cute blue alien Stitch. His unique image didn't come to author and lead animator Chris Sanders right away—the original Stitch had to go through a lot to become the character we know and love. Here's how Stitch was created, and how the character evolved and changed throughout all stages of development. Stitch's birth occurred long before the cartoon hit screens. The character was born as an idea, and the creator of this cute alien was Chris Sanders. At the time, the young man had just graduated from the California Institute of the Arts. The young and inexperienced Chris was offered the opportunity to write a children's book. The book's main character was supposed to be a small creature living in the forest—that was the initial description of the future blue-skinned alien. However, a problem arose: the author was limited by the book's space and couldn't provide a real explanation of where this creature came from. Because of this, Chris abandoned the project—he didn't want to create an incomplete story. To develop this idea, Chris Sanders had to engage in serious brainstorming—over the next three days, he developed a 29-page presentation. In it, he created concepts for future scenes and characters. During this time, the image of Lilo, Stitch's battle buddy, was born. Initially, Sanders wanted to make Lilo a boy, but as the character developed, he realized that a female character would create a more interesting contrast between the two future protagonists. The names for Lilo, and later Nani, also emerged during these three days. During development, Sanders remembered a recent vacation in Hawaii and decided to set the story there. But Chris knew neither the culture nor typical names for the country, so he looked at the roadmap he had made during his vacation—there he found the names Lilo Lane and Nani. The presentation was a success, and Thomas Schumacher approved the project, but stated that the work needed to have a visual style reminiscent of hand-drawn art. Stitch's design development—was he green? After the project was accepted, work began on the character designs and subsequent animation. Several specialists worked on the cartoon, but the team was small—the management didn't get involved until the film was in production. Key figures at the outset were Chris Sanders and Dean DeBlois. Another interesting detail was Stitch's claws. In the drawings and in the film itself, they appear sharp, but if you look closely, you can see that they're filed down. This is why Stitch doesn't scratch or cut—he tears. The reason is that the alien is far from a positive character at the outset, rather aggressive and angry. These claws perfectly complemented his image. The team worked not only on the design but also discussed Stitch's personality. Sandres came up with the idea that Stitch and Lilo resembled each other so much that they could be considered the same character. They share similar backgrounds—both were orphans, forcing them to live in constant fear of being alone. This influenced their personalities—both Lilo and Stitch display aggression and hostility toward others, but to varying degrees. However, there are also differences. According to the story, Stitch was around 30 years old when he met Lilo. All this time, he had to live alone and cope with everything on his own, which ultimately affected his behavior with others. Lilo, on the other hand, was still a little girl, and her personality hadn't yet changed much. Furthermore, Lilo had Nani, who saved her from her crushing loneliness.
